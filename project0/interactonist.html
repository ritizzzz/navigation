Interactonist approach

What is it?

The interactionist approach to language acquisition refers to the idea that language is acquired through an interaction go a human’s innate biological abilities to acquire language, as well as exposure in the environment in which the child is developing. This theory acknowledges that both environmental and biological factors are important in language acquisition

Who proopsed it?

Three of the prominent names in forming the interactionist approach were Lev Vygotsky, Jerome Bruner and Jean Piaget. Vygotsky was a Russian psychologist who created a model of human development which is now referred to as the “sociocultural model”. He believed that there is two distinct stages to cultural development: when children observes adult’s behaviour around them, and then when they begin to interact and participate in this behaviour. Jerome Bruner believe that all humans learn best when they discover knowledge for themselves. He argues that This takes place when babies and adults have “conversations”, even before babies can actually speak. Jean Piaget was the psychologist who first researched and proposed his theory on cognitive development. He proposed that children think and feel differently to adults, and so develop their cognitive skills over time accordingly.

Support

One form of support for this theory is that when children are raised in single parent households are much more likely to say the name of the parent they do have (such as “mama” if raised by just their mothers) Another supporting factor is that when children are very young, they can recognise phonemes from languages all over the world, but slowly lose this ability and begin to only focus on the phonemes in their own language as they are exposed to it.

Criticism

One of the main criticism of this theory is that children still learn to speak, even when they come from cultures that don't promote interaction with babies. An example of this is in Cameroon, where Nso babies are constantly held looking out, rather than at their mothers. This shows that even though the babies don't really interact with their caregiver, they still learn to talk. Another criticism is that many children learn incorrect grammar and syntax, even though they have interacted with adults who do actually speak correctly.
